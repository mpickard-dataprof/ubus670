Part 1: The RCTFC Framework in Action

In the lecture, you learned about the RCTFC framework: Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints. Now you'll see the difference it makes in practice.

1

Your First RCTFC Prompt

Scenario: Beacon's HR department needs a job description for a seasonal retail associate position. You'll build a prompt piece by piece using RCTFC and compare the results to a vague request.

Write a job description for a retail worker.
Role: You are an experienced HR recruiter at a mid-sized retail company in the Midwest. Context: Beacon Retail Group is a 25-store regional retailer headquartered in Rockford, IL. We need to hire 300-400 seasonal workers for the fall rush (September-December). Last year, 42% of seasonal hires didn't complete the season, and we want to improve retention by setting clearer expectations upfront. Task: Write a job description for a Seasonal Retail Associate position. Format: Include these sections: Job Title, Location (multiple Midwest locations), Duration, Key Responsibilities (5-7 bullets), Requirements, What We Offer, and How to Apply. Use a professional but welcoming tone. Constraints: Keep it under 400 words. Do not require prior retail experience. Emphasize the benefits of seasonal work (flexible scheduling, employee discount, potential for permanent hire).
Notice the difference? The first prompt produced a generic template that could apply to any company. The RCTFC prompt produced a tailored job description that addresses Beacon's specific retention problem. That's the power of structured prompting.

Compare with a Classmate

Turn to a neighbor and compare your RCTFC outputs. Did you get the same job description? You probably didn't — and that's important. AI is non-deterministic (like rolling dice — the same input can produce different outputs each time). This is why reviewing AI output is always necessary.

Part 2: Prompting Techniques Showdown

Now that you can structure prompts, let's explore three different techniques for getting better results: zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought prompting.

3

Zero-Shot vs. Few-Shot

Scenario: Beacon's customer service team receives hundreds of emails each week. They need to automatically categorize incoming emails into: Complaint, Question, Return Request, Compliment, or Other.

Categorize the following customer email into one of these categories: Complaint, Question, Return Request, Compliment, Other. Email: "I bought a winter jacket last week and the zipper broke on the first day. This is unacceptable for a $89 jacket. I want my money back or a replacement immediately."
Categorize customer emails for Beacon Retail Group into these categories: Complaint, Question, Return Request, Compliment, Other. Here are examples: Email: "When do your stores close on Thanksgiving?" → Category: Question Email: "I love the new home section! The staff at your Rockford store were so helpful." → Category: Compliment Email: "I need to return a blender I bought online. It's still in the box. What's the process?" → Category: Return Request Now categorize this email: Email: "I bought a winter jacket last week and the zipper broke on the first day. This is unacceptable for a $89 jacket. I want my money back or a replacement immediately."
I ordered the Beacon holiday gift set for my mom (#ORD-4521) but it hasn't arrived yet. The website says delivered but I never got it. I need this resolved before Christmas.
Why this email is tricky: It could be categorized as a Complaint (they're frustrated), a Question (where's my order?), or Other (shipping/delivery issue). Ambiguous cases like this reveal the real difference between zero-shot and few-shot prompting.

Compare with a Classmate

Compare your zero-shot categorization of the ambiguous email with a classmate's. Did the AI give you the same category? Same confidence level? This shows how the same prompt can produce different results — a key reason why AI outputs need human review, especially for edge cases.

4

Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Scenario: Beacon's executive team is considering whether to implement an AI chatbot for customer service. They need a thorough analysis, not just a yes/no answer.

Should Beacon Retail Group implement an AI chatbot for customer service?
Beacon Retail Group receives 850 customer emails per week with a 4-hour average response time. 60% are routine inquiries (store hours, return policy, order status). Think through this step by step: 1. What percentage of emails could an AI chatbot handle? 2. What would be the cost savings (assume a customer service rep costs $18/hour)? 3. What are the risks of using an AI chatbot? 4. What are the risks of NOT implementing one? 5. Based on this analysis, what is your recommendation? Show your reasoning for each step.
Why Chain-of-Thought works: By asking the AI to "think step by step" and providing a reasoning structure, you force the model to break down complex problems rather than jumping to conclusions. This produces more thorough, defensible analysis -- exactly what executives need for decision-making.

Compare with a Classmate

Compare your chain-of-thought chatbot analysis with a classmate's. Did the AI recommend the same thing? Were the cost calculations the same? Even with structured step-by-step prompts, AI can reach different conclusions from the same data. This is why AI analysis should inform decisions, not make them.

Part 3: The Iteration Cycle

Great prompts rarely happen on the first try. Professional prompt engineers use an iterative process: generate, evaluate, refine, repeat. In this section, you'll practice that cycle.

5

Iterative Refinement

Scenario: Beacon needs a training manual outline for their 300-400 incoming seasonal workers. The onboarding program is only 2 days, so every minute counts.

Create a training manual outline for seasonal retail workers at Beacon Retail Group.
Revise the training manual outline with these additions: - Include a section on Beacon's return policy (30-day returns, receipt required, gift receipts accepted) - Add POS system basics (our system is called BeaconPOS) - Include loss prevention awareness - Keep it achievable in a 2-day onboarding program - Format as a day-by-day schedule
The Iteration Cycle: Generate → Evaluate → Specify → Regenerate. Each round should add one or two specific improvements, not rewrite everything. Think of it like sculpting: start with the rough shape, then refine the details.
Common mistake: Don't try to fix everything in one prompt. If you dump ten changes at once, the AI may lose track of some. Iterate in small, focused steps for better results.
