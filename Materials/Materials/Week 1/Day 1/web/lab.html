Part 3: The Hallucination Hunt

Now let's intentionally trigger a hallucination to see how AI can confidently state incorrect information. Modern AI models have become better at admitting uncertainty, so we'll try multiple approaches.

6

Trigger a Hallucination

Try each of these approaches until you catch a hallucination:

Approach A: Ask for Research Citations

Ask Gemini to cite specific research to support a claim:

What peer-reviewed academic studies have been published about the effectiveness of AI in retail hiring? Please provide specific paper titles, authors, and publication years.

Approach B: Ask for Specific Statistics

Request precise numbers without providing sources:

What is the exact average cost-per-hire for seasonal retail workers in the Midwest United States as of 2025? Include the specific dollar amount and the source of this data.

Approach C: Fictional Company Test

Ask about our fictional company as if it were real:

Tell me about Beacon Retail Group, the Midwest retail chain founded in Rockford, Illinois. Who founded it? When? How many stores do they have?
‚ö†Ô∏è Note: Beacon Retail Group is fictional‚Äîwe created it for this course. Modern AI might:
  • Admit it doesn't know (good!)
  • Confuse it with a real company
  • Make up plausible-sounding but completely false details
üí° Key Insight: AI models are getting better at saying "I don't know," but they still hallucinate‚Äîespecially with specific statistics, citations, and unfamiliar topics. This is why you should NEVER trust AI output for critical business decisions without human verification.
